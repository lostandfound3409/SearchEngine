10 Talking Points From The Dark Knight Rises’ Data Dump

\t\t\t
\n\n\t
\n\n\t\t
\n\t\t\t

Until now, tech-noir auteur Christopher Nolan has kept the finer points of his highly anticipated The Dark Knight Rises on the down-low. Then came the nearly 50-page data dump.

\n

We’ve sifted through The Dark Knight Rises‘ dense production notes, released earlier this week, and seized upon the intriguing data points below. From awkward politics and interesting technical details to geeky comics tangles, they help brighten the Bat-Signal shining on Nolan’s final Batman blockbuster, which opens July 20. Here’s some insight into what has, up to this point, been a purposefully shadowy operation.

\n

\n

1. Camera Upgrades Mean a Darker Dark Knight Rises

\n

Nolan filmed almost half the film — including its biggest battles — using large-format Imax cameras and high-resolution 15 perf/65-mm film for maximum clarity and depth. Cinematographer Wally Pfister praised the immersive picture and sound afforded by the format, and revealed that upgrades allowed him to capture extraordinary imagery.

\n

“We spent about six months working with Panavision and Imax to retool the viewfinder on the cameras and craft new lenses, which allowed us to shoot in very low-light conditions,” Pfister said in the production notes. “With those advancements, we were able to do things we couldn’t do on the previous film.”

\n

Noland said 102 screens will show the original Imax film photography “in its optimum form” and encouraged Bat-fans to seek out those theaters. “I appreciate what it offers from the technical side, but I’m most interested in what it can give me as a storytelling tool,” Nolan said. “Imax provides the broadest possible canvas.”

\n

2.Terrorism Gets Retconned

\n

Terrorism is a word so abused that it barely means anything anymore, which is why you hear it to describe things like teachers’ unions and journalists. Expect The Dark Knight Rises to step into that quagmire.

\n

“Bane is a terrorist in both his mentality and his actions,” said Tom Hardy, who plays The Dark Knight Rises‘ supervillain. “He is physically intimidating and he’s also very intelligent, which makes him even more dangerous.” Actor Christian Bale, who dons the cowl for Nolan again, said Bane is “ruthless in terms of his sheer militancy and the ideology that drives him.”

\n

\n\n

3. Gotham Gets Occupied

\n

There are already rumbles in the Twitterverse calling bullshit on The Dark Knight Rises’ relationship to the Occupy movement. “The new Batman movie is full of anti-\xe2\x80\xaa#OWS\xe2\x80\xac stuff,” tweeted The Coup’s Boots Riley. “The big protest [movement] against the rich is controlled by an evil Russian villain.”

\n

Bane’s not Russian in the comics, but instead hails from a fake Caribbean island called Santa Prisca, where he led a hardscrabble life. In other words, Bane is more than likely a 99-percenter. But Bruce Wayne? “The least relatable thing about him is his billionaire status,” Bale said. “Having those kinds of resources is something very few people could ever fathom.”

\n

One of The Dark Knight Rises’ greatest battles takes place outside a stock exchange. (Production shut down Wall Street over two weekends.) Looking for another Occupy analog? Check out the big bad’s theme music. “We went in a completely different direction for Bane,” said composer Hans Zimmer. “I wanted to use a big symphony orchestra, but I said to them, ‘I’m going to make you unlearn everything you’ve learned. I’m going to treat you as if you were a primeval drum circle.’ And it turned out to be very liberating for them.”

\n

4. Holy Crappy Social Engineering, Batman!

\n

The plot of The Dark Knight Rises hinges on the Dent Act, a draconian anti-crime conspiracy created by Batman and Commissioner Gordon in honor of the Joker-ruined idealist Harvey Dent, whose death as the horrific alter ego Two-Face closed out The Dark Knight.

\n

The Dent Act is a total bust, having pushed Gotham’s criminal element to the fringe, where it has gained malevolence over the past eight years. “The Dark Knight is no longer needed in Gotham,” said Nolan. “In that regard, Bruce Wayne has won the battle, but he is traumatized by what happened and doesn’t know how to move on from being the figure of Batman.”

\n

5. Dark Knight Rises Geeks Should Read Knightfall First

\n

Before Nolan’s film, Bane was mostly a benchwarmer in Batman’s rogues gallery, although the supervillain infamously broke the Bat in the comic miniseries Knightfall. (The character eventually became far cooler as a beast of burdened conscience during Gail Simone’s twisted, delirious Secret Six run.)

\n

Nolan needed someone very physical to empower his brute psychodrama, but you can expect Bane to exhibit the type of intellectual range that makes him capable of matching wits with Batman — just like in Knightfall, where he was the mastermind behind a plot to take down the Dark Knight. Nolan called the face-to-face confrontation between the two characters the centerpiece of the film.

\n

“Bane has come to do a job and has no feelings of remorse or shame about the death and destruction he’s causing,” said Hardy. “There is nothing ambiguous about Bane. He is clearly a villain … just a horrible piece of work.”

\n

6. Joseph Gordon-Levitt Sure Looks Like Robin

\n

The official word is that Joseph Gordon-Levitt plays idealistic Gotham City police officer John Blake, a straight shooter who impresses Gordon. But picturing the actor in Robin’s shoes gets easier as the movie nears, despite the fact that this is Nolan’s last bow with the Bat. (Gordon-Levitt could also be Azrael, who fans of the comic Knightfall know takes over for Wayne as Batman for a spell.)

\n

\n\n

7. Anne Hathaway Fills Out Catwoman’s Spandex

\n

It’s Hathaway’s first time working with Nolan, who has a tendency to pick winners. That means Catwoman fetishists high on Michelle Pfeiffer’s purring PTSD from Batman Returns and Julie Newmar’s vamping from campy ’60s television series Batman better brace themselves for a more calculating character stuffed into the skintight catsuit. (The less said about Halle Berry, the better.)

\n

“Her morally ambiguous philosophy finally gives Batman someone he can relate to,” said the director’s screenwriting brother Jonathan Nolan about Hathaway’s turn as Catwoman. “In a weird way, she’s the yin to his yang. The dynamic between them is so fresh — the playful way she kind of pokes fun at him — it sparks a connection between them and takes some of the somberness away from his character.”

\n

Hathaway said she read up on “some archival comics” and Bob Kane’s inspiration for Catwoman to prep for the role. “But the most important thing was to be Catwoman in this film and fit into Christopher Nolan’s Gotham City,” she said.

\n

8. What, No Bill Finger?

\n

Batman looked more like Superman before artist Bill Finger took writer Bob Kane’s conception of the superheroic avenger and ran with it. You can thank Finger for taking the red out of the Dark Knight, as well as for providing the character with much of his timeless iconography. None of this backstory is mentioned in the production notes, which cites both DC Comics and Kane for creating the character, but omits Finger. It’s the latest in a long line of omissions and slights when it comes to crediting the creators of timeless comics heroes.

\n

9. Good News! No Bat-Nipples!

\n

Don’t expect anything like the ridiculous modifications to the batsuit that ruined previous Batman sequels. “If it ain’t broke, don’t fix it,” said costume designer Lindy Hemming, who assembled 110 separate pieces atop a type of base polyester mesh used by the military and professional athletes. But as any fan of Batman comics knows, the suit really doesn’t matter. It’s the mind and the man beneath it that does.

\n

“When Christian put on that suit, I didn’t have to make believe I was talking to Batman,” said Gordon-Levitt. “It doesn’t happen very often, but every now and then when I’m shooting a movie, I have a moment when it feels like real life. That was definitely one of the strongest of those moments I’ve ever had.”

\n

10. The Bat Takes Flight, So to Speak

\n

Gearheads should be stoked that The Dark Knight Rises is moving beyond the franchise’s mostly underwhelming Tumbler and Bat-Pod, even if it’s just to a Bat-plane called … The Bat. Like the Tumbler, The Bat literally borrows from military hardware — in this case, the Apache attack helicopter, the Osprey prop jet and the Harrier jump jet.

\n

“There are a lot of moving parts: The cockpit opens; the aerial flaps all operate; and it has working rotors and lights,” said special effects supervisor Chris Corbould. “It’s nearly 30 feet long and 17 feet wide and weighs about 3,000 pounds; it’s a big piece of machinery. We employed a variety of different methodologies to make The Bat ‘fly,’ because Chris’s mantra is he wants to do as much as possible before letting CGI take over. So we had it supported on wires, running along high lines, suspended from cranes or helicopters, and mounted on a specially constructed vehicle fitted with hydraulic controls.”

\n

But there was one problem: The Bat couldn’t actually take off on its own, a blow to Batman realism that only obsessive geeks will likely cry about.

\n

“To get something like that off the ground was beyond our capabilities,” said Corbould. I’d be a very rich man if I could build something like that that could actually fly.”
