\n
\n
\n

JD Salinger wrote The Catcher In The Rye in 1951. It was originally published for adults but its themes of teenage angst and alienation saw it gain a new audience with adolescent readers. Written in a subjective style from the point of view of its protagonist and antihero Holden Caulfield, following his exact thought process. Holden Caulfield has since become an icon for teenage rebellion. Between 1961 and 1982, The Catcher in the Rye was the most censored book in high schools and libraries in the United States. But it was also the second most taught book in American schools. In 2005, the novel was included on Time Magazine\'s list of the 100 best English language novels written since 1923.

    I have to protest at the recorded promotion for this programme, in which Michael Cathcart refers to the novel as "Catcher in the Rye". Henry James didn\'t write "Ambassadors"; he wrote "The Ambassadors". Charles Dickens didn\'t write "Christmas Carol"; he wrote "A Christmas Carol". Definite and indefinite articles in book titles are important, and their omission can completely change the meaning of a title or render it absurd.

    David Huggett :

    \n\t

    25 Sep 2013 10:05:18am

    \n\t

    I only recently read The Catcher In The Rye, and the question which leapt off every page for me was what sort of father would engender such a jaundiced dysfunctional youth as Holden Caulfield. Holden\'s neatly described behaviour and attitudes all seemed to me to show him just helplessly emulating his father and unable to find himself.
    The tantalising expectation which was almost fulfilled near the end of the book was that we would eventually meet the man whose neglect and undertone of abuse was responsible for Caulfield\'s psychopathy. But alas, it didn\xe2\x80\x99t happen, we were just left to speculate on what an unpleasant and revealing encounter it would have been.
    In other words, I think this novel is a prophetic parable describing the degeneration of the \'American Dream\' which the father embodied and which Holden was reluctantly adopting, as a result of not having the spiritual and mental resilience to resist.

The other\xe2\x80\x99s comments were wonderful really but in comparison they somehow did feel a little bit \xe2\x80\x9cphoney\xe2\x80\x9d. On first reading the book after long hearing it mentioned by many, I was disappointed; and like Stellar I do not like Holden. It reminded me too much of empty, apathetic, wasted years of high school where I learnt and did so little. After high school I was lucky to stumble on the joy and fulfilment of a full engagement with life and people but there was no prospect of that kind of growth in Holden that I could see.
    The book does reflect well the disaster that our high school system is for the creativity and inspiration of so many. I think the problem with Holden is not that he is lazy, as Stellar put it, but that he has been poorly nurtured to be the engaged, joyfully thriving teenager that is all our birth potential; by family, community and probably as much as any by high school. This is what I would have loved to hear Stellar comment on: does she see peers going through the same; for those that aren\xe2\x80\x99t is it due to better nurture along the way or a better high school structure perhaps? Big questions for one so young perhaps but this is perhaps the utility, I am now beginning to think, the book, and such discussion could promote. Catcher in the Rye reminds me of the ennui of Tolstoys early writings in \xe2\x80\x9cChildhood and Youth\xe2\x80\x9d; He followed that with the brilliantly inspiring and life affirming characters of \xe2\x80\x9cWar and Peace\xe2\x80\x9d and Levin and Kitty and others in \xe2\x80\x9cAnna Karenina\xe2\x80\x9d. It\xe2\x80\x99s a great pity that Salinger has not done something similar.
