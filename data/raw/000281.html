The internet went crazy yesterday with\xc2\xa0news that Madonna had completed work on her 13th studio album. In the wee hours of Monday morning,\xc2\xa0Fashion photographers\xc2\xa0Mert and Marcus posted a picture of the Queen\xc2\xa0on\xc2\xa0Instagram, along with the caption:\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn my room listening to the NEW ALBUM!!!!! Im DYING!!!!!! LET ME LOVE YOU FROM INSIDE OUT ???????? @madonna\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

There’s no denying Madge’s greatness has diminished somewhat in recent years, but we’re not convinced she’s completely lost her magic touch. We know there’s still a masterpiece\xc2\xa0or two left in her. And we can’t wait to hear what she has in store.

\n

In anticipation of her new\xc2\xa0release, we’ve ranked all 12 of the Material Girl’s\xc2\xa0albums in order of greatness. We can’t help but wonder where her 13th effort will fall on our chart.

\n

Scroll down to see our official\xc2\xa0ranking. And let us know where you think\xc2\xa0in the comments section below.

\n

12.\xc2\xa0American Life\xc2\xa0(2003)

\n

\n

It’s probably safe to say that everyone — including Madonna — would like to forget that 2003’s\xc2\xa0American Life\xc2\xa0ever happened. The Kabbalah-inspired acoustic/electronica disaster\xc2\xa0features\xc2\xa0Madge/Esther awkwardly rapping\xc2\xa0about “drinking a soy latte” and “getting a double shot-ey” that “goes right through her bod-ey,” among other things. The album\xc2\xa0failed to spawn any\xc2\xa0memorial singles\xc2\xa0and remains her lowest-selling\xc2\xa0to date.

\n

11.\xc2\xa0MDNA\xc2\xa0(2012)

\n

\n

Perhaps no other Madonna album inspires as much divide among fans as 2012’s MDNA.\xc2\xa0Some love it, others loathe it.\xc2\xa0This was Madonna’s\xc2\xa0first album\xc2\xa0post-Lady Gaga, post-divorce from\xc2\xa0Guy Richie, and post-menopause. Turns out those three things combined were a recipe for disaster, which, in hindsight, really isn’t all that surprising. MDNA\xc2\xa0was panned by critics, suffered one of the largest sales drops in Billboard history between its first and second weeks,\xc2\xa0had virtually no impact on radio, and was even trashed by its own producer, William Orbit. In all fairness, had it been released by another pop artist — perhaps someone younger and less established — it may have fared better. But coming from an icon like Madonna, it was, put simply, a disappointment.

\n

10.\xc2\xa0Hard Candy\xc2\xa0(2008)

\n

\n

In\xc2\xa0its\xc2\xa0defense, 2008’s\xc2\xa0Hard Candy\xc2\xa0had big shoes to fill. It\xc2\xa0followed\xc2\xa0one of the Material Girl’s\xc2\xa0strongest late-career albums to date,\xc2\xa0Confessions on a Dance Floor,\xc2\xa0as well as her critically-acclaimed, sold-out world tour. Miss Ciccone\xc2\xa0paired up with producers Timbaland, Pharrell Williams and Justin Timberlake in hopes of cashing in on their popular, of-the-moment sound. The only problem: She was about two years behind the trend.\xc2\xa0Hard Candy\xc2\xa0sounded dated before it was even released. On top of that, it featured one of\xc2\xa0the\xc2\xa0most cringe-worthy songs from Madge’s\xc2\xa0entire\xc2\xa0catalog,\xc2\xa0Spanish Lesson, in which\xc2\xa0she assaults listeners’ ears with incorrect Spanish-to-English translations for three and a half minutes. We still haven’t totally forgiven her for that.

\n

9.\xc2\xa0Bedtime Stories\xc2\xa0(1994)

\n

\n

While we love 1994’s mid-tempo, R&B-infused\xc2\xa0Bedtime Stories\xc2\xa0(it makes for great sex music), the cold, hard truth is, it didn’t have\xc2\xa0nearly\xc2\xa0the same impact as many of Madonna’s other efforts. Sure it\xc2\xa0spawned one of the biggest singles of her career (Take A Bow\xc2\xa0spent\xc2\xa0seven weeks at #1) as well as the iconic S&M-themed Human Nature\xc2\xa0music video, but in terms of\xc2\xa0cultural causatum, it didn’t offer\xc2\xa0much. Not even a tour. But we heart it anyway.

\n

8.\xc2\xa0Like A Virgin\xc2\xa0(1984)

\n

\n

We debated listing\xc2\xa0Like A Virgin\xc2\xa0at #8 since it was an\xc2\xa0important\xc2\xa0album in Madonna’s career and we love it to death. It features two of her most memorable early hits \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0“Like A Virgin”\xc2\xa0and\xc2\xa0“Material Girl”\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0and\xc2\xa0solidified her status as a cultural\xc2\xa0force to be reckoned with. However, if we’re being really honest, the album kinda sounds like an extension of her previous one \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa01983’s\xc2\xa0Madonna \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0only without as many good songs. Like A Virgin\xc2\xa0has as many\xc2\xa0mediocre\xc2\xa0tracks as it does good ones. Does anybody even sing along to\xc2\xa0“Shoo-Bee-Doo,” “Over and Over,”\xc2\xa0“Love Don’t Live Here Anymore”\xc2\xa0or “Pretender”\xc2\xa0anymore?

\n

7.\xc2\xa0Erotica\xc2\xa0(1992)

\n

\n

When Madonna’s fifth studio album\xc2\xa0Erotica\xc2\xa0(or as we like to call it Eroti-ca)\xc2\xa0was first released in 1992, it shocked the world. So much so that it was banned in several countries. Critics were divided over the album’s quality. Some\xc2\xa0called\xc2\xa0it\xc2\xa0the pop star’s\xc2\xa0most ambitious to date. Others\xc2\xa0said it was\xc2\xa0nothing more than\xc2\xa0pornography for the ears. The album contains one of our all-time favorite songs, the bitch-slapping, club-banging\xc2\xa0Thief of Hearts.\xc2\xa0Seriously, why wasn’t that track made into a single?

\n

6.\xc2\xa0Music\xc2\xa0(2000)

\n

\n

2000’s\xc2\xa0Music\xc2\xa0followed Madonna’s multi-Grammy\xc2\xa0Award-winning magnum opus\xc2\xa0Ray of Light,\xc2\xa0and, frankly,\xc2\xa0she\xc2\xa0couldn’t have done a better job.\xc2\xa0After the whole Earth-Mother-Spiritual-Y0ga-Goddess phase, she\xc2\xa0decided to let her hair down and have a little fun, and the end result was 44 minutes of pure pop perfection.\xc2\xa0This is the album (and supporting tour) that made Madonna the official Queen of Pop.\xc2\xa0Music\xc2\xa0offers the perfect balance of pop songs, dance tracks, and mid-tempo ballads that still make for a throughly enjoyable listen nearly 15 years later.

\n

5.\xc2\xa0True Blue\xc2\xa0(1987)

\n

\n

This album single-handedly defines ’80s music and remains one of the best-selling albums of all time more than 25 years after it was released. With songs like “Papa Don’t Preach,” “Open Your Heart,” “La Isla Bonita,” “Live to Tell”\xc2\xa0and “True Blue,”\xc2\xa0Madonna’s third studio effort\xc2\xa0is jam-packed with hits. It’s no surprise it reached #1 in 28 countries and spent 34 consecutive weeks at the top of the\xc2\xa0European Top 100 Albums\xc2\xa0chart, longer than any other album in history. It’s one of Madonna’s most pure fun\xc2\xa0albums and we\xc2\xa0continue to blast it regularly to this day.

\n

4.\xc2\xa0Confessions on a Dance Floor\xc2\xa0(2005)

\n

\n

Perhaps her last truly\xc2\xa0great artistic achievement to date, Confessions on a Dance Floor\xc2\xa0is Madonna’s late-career, disco-infused, infectious masterpiece. It’s the album that reminded us why we fell in love with her\xc2\xa0in the first place. The Queen paired up with producer Stuart Price to create 12 dance songs that flowed seamlessly together. Then she slipped into a pink leotard and roller skates and started grinding on a boombox. Seriously, how could anyone\xc2\xa0not love\xc2\xa0everything about that?\xc2\xa0Our only criticism is the song “I Love New York,” in which Madge rhymes “New York” with “dork.” But by 2005, a bad rhyme or questionable lyric here and there had become\xc2\xa0a staple item in every Madonna record.

\n

3.\xc2\xa0Ray of Light\xc2\xa0(1998)

\n

\n

Oh boy, we’re down to the top three… There’s no denying\xc2\xa0Ray of Light was Madonna’s most inspired album \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0lyrically, musically, thematically. It’s also the album that won her her first Grammy. And it\xc2\xa0proved that\xc2\xa0she\xc2\xa0could actually \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0well \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0sing. Her\xc2\xa0voice was in top form after\xc2\xa0Evita.\xc2\xa0Madonna has often\xc2\xa0called the\xc2\xa0Ray of Light-era her favorite, and we can understand why. It’s the album\xc2\xa0that finally got people to\xc2\xa0take her seriously as a musician. It’s also the album she’s never been quite able to match. Was it just a stroke of genius? Or does she\xc2\xa0have another\xc2\xa0Ray of Light\xc2\xa0in her somewhere?

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Like A Prayer\xc2\xa0(1989)

\n

\n

The title track alone is what lands\xc2\xa0Like A Prayer in the #2 spot of our list. To this day, every time it comes on the radio, we can’t help but turn it up. The album, too, is pretty damn good. Madonna tackles some\xc2\xa0rather\xc2\xa0lofty\xc2\xa0themes \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0the Catholic church, her divorce from Sean Penn, her mother’s death \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0but songs like\xc2\xa0Express Yourself and\xc2\xa0Cherish keep the mood from getting too heavy. All in all,\xc2\xa0Like A Prayer is probably one of her most personal albums. And it achieves subtle greatness without trying too hard.

\n

1.\xc2\xa0Madonna (1983)

\n

\n

In the beginning, there was\xc2\xa0Madonna.\xc2\xa0The Queen’s self-titled debut comes in at #1 on our list because \xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0well \xe2\x80\x94 it’s the album that started it all. Without\xc2\xa0Madonna there would be no Madonna. On top of that, the songs are pretty dope. This is the album that brought us “Holiday,” “Lucky Star,” “Burning Up”\xc2\xa0and “Borderline.”\xc2\xa0But, truthfully, every song is good. It’s crazy to think this album turned 30 last year. Here’s to 30 more!\xc2\xa0L’chaim!

      I Want You, I’ll Remember (which was the theme and song from my senior prom for which I had major influence since I was senior class president), Take a Bow, This Used to be my Playground, Something to Remember, and Rain are my favorites from that album. I just love that album so much.
