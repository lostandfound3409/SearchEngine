A Tale of Two Cities Quotes

\n\t\t\t\t\t

by Saba Seperteladze · December 29, 2014

\n\t\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\t\n\n\n\n\t
\n\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t
\n\t\n\n\n\n\n\t\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\t
\t\n\t\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t

What a book! After reading this, I’ve started to appreciate Dickens much more than “that guy who wrote the Christmas Carol.” Actually it truly is my favorite Dickens book, which I have read! It inculeds love, sacrifice, revenge and other exciting/inspiring stuff! I’m a big fan of a solid marriage between character development and action. Some criticize Dickens for his trite stories and overblown caricature-esque characters. I know, I know, the man wrote some less than a perfect books. He had been writing them for a wide-ranging public and for money. But here you have the author at his finest, creating sympathetic charachters and plotting a riveting tale. The descriptions of the rebellion is interesting, but it’s the dual nature of the revolutionaries that I really appreciate. He makes you feel for their plight and then twists the plot, so that the tortured \xc2\xa0become the tyrants and your fondness turns to loathing as you witness their despicable deeds. Dickens put a lot of effort and feeling into A Tale of Two Cities.
\n

\n

Read 10\xc2\xa0A Tale of Two Cities Quotes :

\n

1.A wonderful fact to reflect upon, that every human creature is constituted to be that profound secret and mystery to every other.
\n

\n

2.It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done; it is a far, far better rest that I go to than I have ever known.
\n

\n

3.It was the best of times, it was the worst of times.
\n

\n

4.It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of light, it was the season of darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair.
\n

\n

5.For you, and for any dear to you, I would do anything. If my career were of that better kind that there was any opportunity or capacity of sacrifice in it, I would embrace any sacrifice for you and for those dear to you. Try to hold me in your mind, at some quiet times, as ardent and sincere in this one thing. The time will come, the time will not be long in coming, when new ties will be formed about you–ties that will bind you yet more tenderly and strongly to the home you so adorn–the dearest ties that will ever grace and gladden you. O Miss Manette, when the little picture of a happy father’s face looks up in yours, when you see your own bright beauty springing up anew at your feet, think now and then that there is a man who would give his life, to keep a life you love beside you.
\n

\n

6.There is prodigious strength in sorrow and despair.
\n

\n

7.And yet I have had the weakness, and have still the weakness, to wish you to know with what a sudden mastery you kindled me, heap of ashes that I am, into fire.
\n

\n

8.Sadly, sadly, the sun rose; it rose upon no sadder sight than the man of good abilities and good emotions, incapable of their directed exercise, incapable of his own help and his own happiness, sensible of the blight on him, and resigning himself to let it eat him away.
\n

\n

9.A day wasted on others is not wasted on one’s self.
\n

\n

10.Think now and then that there is a man who would give his life, to keep a life you love beside you.
\n
