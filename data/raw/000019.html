The Little Prince by Antoine de Saint Exupéry

\r\n \r\n
\r\n\tMay 28, 2014 5:16 pm , 2 Comments , Ashleigh
\r\n \r\n \r\n
\t\r\n\t

Antoine de Saint Exup\xc3\xa9ry’s classic children’s novel has surprisingly a lot to say about what it means to be an adult.

\n

One sees clearly only with the heart. What is essential is invisible to the eye.

\n

I am on a roll this week, because I read not only one, but two books! This definitely makes me worthy of the biggest gold star ever. Though, to be fair, the second book I read was a children’s book\xe2\x80\x94The Little Prince, by Antoine de Saint Exup\xc3\xa9ry.

\n

This book is considered a children’s classic but, for one reason or another, I myself never picked\xc2\xa0it up as a kid (I was probably\xc2\xa0too busy reading Junie B. Jones\xc2\xa0or Harry Potter or something Beverly Cleary). It came to me from a friend who, upon hearing I had never read it, brought me his own copy to read, the book so old that the pages are yellowing and soft from being turned\xc2\xa0over and over. Already the pressure is on, since sharing books is, in my opinion, a highly intimate and personal experience; it’s like someone is sharing a part of themselves with you, and you want to fully appreciate and honor their trust in letting you glimpse into their life. Knowing the book was fairly short and had plenty of pictures woven in with its chapters, I started almost immediately.

\n

The story-within-a-story tells the tale of a pilot stranded in the desert who encounters the little prince, a boy who has fallen from an asteroid. The bulk of the book is the little prince recounting his experiences traveling through space and on Earth, as he comes along several allegorical characters (such as a king, a businessman, a conceited man…etc.). Each encounter is seen through childlike bewilderment at the absurdity of adulthood, and the paradoxes\xc2\xa0the little prince sees between what these adults say and do. He also befriends\xc2\xa0a fox, who teaches him what it means to be tamed (“to establish ties”) and how we establish these ties to even the most ordinary, common things. Other allegorical elements are the little prince’s planet, which he is the sole habitant, as well as a rose that drifts to his planet, which he comes to find great affection for after taking care of her.

\n

Though the story is, technically, a children’s book, it was surprisingly relevant to me as an adult (or however much being a 21-year old constitutes being\xc2\xa0an adult). It was whimsical and touching, and made me think about the person who I wanted to be as a child and who I’ve actually grown up to be. I was especially fond of the encounters with the fox and the rose, as they spoke more to me about what it is to have people come and go from your life, and how best to love them.

\n

And, of course, I discovered by the first page that this was the book with the famous “boa constrictor eating an elephant” drawing, which became the narrator’s test to see whether or not someone had succumbed to adulthood:

\n

\n

 

\n

Overall, I’m glad this book made its way into my hands; it was a charming\xc2\xa0reminder to take a step back and look at what is most important in living one’s life. It felt like it came at just the right time…funny, how books find a way of doing that. \xc2\xa0And though I didn’t get to read it as a child, I’m sure it will stick with me\xc2\xa0as an adult.
