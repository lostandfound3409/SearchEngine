Best movie experience of the last 30 years...

\n
\nAuthor:\nBalazs Jedovszky (jedighost@gmail.com) from Hungary
\n14 December 2009
\n

*** This review may contain spoilers ***

\r\n\n
\n

\nI was lucky to see Avatar at a pre-screening a few hours ago. It\ncompletely blew me and the whole room away and i dare to say it will do\nso to 80% of any audience anywhere. The remaining 20%, who always finds\nsomething to complain about, will whine about character development,\ndialog, story or the pop-corn.

Well, let me tell you: they went to this movie with the wrong\nexpectations.

You have most likely met Cameron's previous work(s): Aliens, Terminator\n1 & 2, The Abyss, Titanic (!), just to name a few.

So WHAT should you expect from Avatar??? MORE of the same!!! More of\nrevolutionary film-making, more of grandiose new ideas, more of\nnever-before-seen special effects, more of 150 minutes without\nrelapsing, more of the James Cameron genius...

I am very happy that the trailers didn't give the full story away. Lots\nof emotions are waiting for the viewer, laughter and tears also.\nCameron was very smart keeping the teasers as teasers, nothing more...\nas the full movie will take your breath away.

You will practically not notice that you are watching a non-existing\nworld, it is sooo real. Attention to detail is superb. You computer\ngeeks will know what I am talking about. This move was not rushed in\nthe making. No wonder it could not have been done before - not having\nthe proper computing power.

The wild life, the jungle, the animals, the Na'vi-s, or the dragon-like\nflying creatures are all so life-like, they almost pop-off the screen\n(and in 3D they actually do :) .

The sound effects were so well done, that when I saw at the credits\nthat Skywalker Sound was behind it, i could only think of "yessss...\nnow THAT makes sense."

Cameron is a visionaire - and again, he delivers, with full blast.

A certain character says in the movie that Pandora (the planet where\nthe story takes place) became his real world. My advice to you: let\nPandora become YOUR real world for two and a half hours, let it make\nyou completely forget about your life and problems, let it entertain\nyou, move you, let it carry you away.

Because THAT is what i expect as an exchange for my ticket.

And a few hours ago i got tens of tickets worth of that.\n

Technically outstanding. Originality: oh well....

\n\nAuthor:\nelchocobollo from Spain
\n11 December 2009
\n\n
\n

\nWell, I just saw Avatar this morning, one of the press premieres which\nare running on these days. My opinion: you've seen this story a hundred\ntimes, but never like this. Finally 3D is what it's supposed to be, an\ninstrument at the service of the movie. You'll enjoy the visual\nexperience, no doubt.

As for the story, some of the "inspirations" are so huge and so obvious\nthat mentioning two or three of them would REALLY ruin the movie for\nyou, and I'm not willing to do that. Lots of mysticism and ecology, if\nyou like that stuff. If you're 15 or so, you'll have a great time\nthinking that it's the first time somebody makes something like this.\nIf you're an experienced movie watcher, better leave your skepticism at\nthe door, bring lots of pop corn and enjoy with the usual\naction-flick-with-moral-and-loads-of-clichés.

I liked it, however: "the movie that re-invents movies"??? No way.\n

\n
\nAuthor:\nMisagh from Earth

*** This review may contain spoilers ***

\r\n\n
\n

\nI had the rather intense privilege to view James Cameron's much\nanticipated $400 million budget return to the directing scene, Avatar,\nat the Empire Leicester Square in London.

Where to begin! The visuals in this pieces was groundbreaking. He did\nit with the Terminator series and then Titanic, so one would expect\nCameron to deliver... and HE DID! The visual are by far some of the\nsharpest CGI I have seen. You could almost say that there is a disquiet\nthat follows Cameron's soul, as there is no other possibility of this\nstrong and intensified quality. Its production design and visual\neffects are both noteworthy and it will get its praise upon official\nrelease.

What it was lacking that really should have shaped the movie is its\ncharacter/story. I was expecting a complex and believable plot, but was\nleft with a movie with mostly strong visuals. What most sci-fi lovers\ndesire is mind-bending philosophies, fantasy and exploration and\nlimitations of our or outer species. If it was not for this factor, I\nwould give this a 9.5 vote.

Avatar will be a success, not only because of Cameron's legacy, but by\nvery intelligent and viral marketing. Avatar have had a powerful\nmarketing technique that assembles other successful blockbusters, such\nas The Blair Witch project (you all remember it), The Dark Knight\n(Joker invades the world) and also, the current production The Artifice\n(the-artifice.com) that is intelligently targeting the market.

Kudos to Cameron, Avatar is one of the (if not The) movie of the year.\nI could get in trouble for sharing this with you guys so early, so\nplease click Yes on "Was the above comment useful to you?" as a thank\nyou. ;)\n

As Writer/Director, James Cameron deserves high praise for this\ncreation and in my opinion it will break box office records. I\nthoroughly enjoyed this film.\n

\n\n

One of the best cinematic experiences ever...

\n
\nAuthor:\nClayton Davis (Claytondavis@awardscircuit.com) from New Jersey
\n14 December 2009
\n\n
\n

\nForgive me, I'm going to jump from professional to fan boy for a while\nhere. I haven't had the jitters after a film the way I've had for\nAvatar in quite sometime. James Cameron's Avatar is the most\nentertaining and enthralling cinematic experiences of my life. It is\nincredible, simply put. What Cameron has done here is the most\npassionate film project put out since Steven Spielberg released\nSchindler's List. His attention to detail and his zeal for pushing the\nenvelope is so admirable to any filmmaker or actor who will ever do\nanother film from this point on.

Avatar is the story of Jake Sully, a paraplegic marine, who replaces\nhis brother on a secret mission to infiltrate the Na' vi, the colony of\nbeings that sit on the planet of Pandora, where there is a precious\nore, that sells at a ridiculous amount. When Jake learns the ways of\nthe Na' vi, his feelings and learnings will put him and the people he\ntrusts in dangerous jeopardy.

The performances here, in the sense of reacting, becoming, and\nunderstanding what Cameron has written are astounding. Not to be\nconfused with a sensational bravura performance from some of the\ncenturies best such as Marion Brando, Tom Hanks, or Diane Keaton; these\nactors along with the director inhabit these visual transformations\nwith special effects as if they are have lived these beings all their\nlives. This is all based on character movements and reactions. Sam\nWorthington, as Jake Sully, is an actor who's on his way to becoming a\nstar. Though he has problems with his Aussie accent often enough in the\nfilm, he gets the job done. Zoe Saldana, who plays Neytiri, a Na' vi\nhuntress, is thrilling and electrifying. Stephen Lang, as the rock hard\nColonel Miles, takes on a villainous turn to a new level in science\nfiction. He offers actual emotion and emotes evil to the audience and\ngains our hatred easily. Sigourney Weaver as the beautiful Dr. Grace,\nis sufficient enough to have on screen again teamed with Cameron. She\nlives inside her role with effortless ease, but suffers from some of\nthe typical James Cameron cheesy lines.

Narratively the film works perfectly on the cinematic level. The first\nforty minutes or so require patience and hope as it is the weakest part\nof the film and offers some dreariness, but when the second act takes\noff, it's sky high with no limits for James Cameron. Avatar delivers\nthe best action sequences put on film of all time. That is the boldest\nstatement I have ever made in all my years of criticism. I sat on this\nfor two days before charging it out, but I mean it. It is the best\nvisual experience of my life, period.

Other than those visuals, the film pops with all the other technical\naspects thrown into one. Art Direction is killer as the two worlds\nblend in perfectly for an acceptable time. The Film Editing is the\ncrowning achievement of the film as it also offers the perfect blend of\nthe two worlds, enticing the viewer and shifting us around. Mauro Fiore\nis the threat for a Cinematography Oscar this year. It was if the\nviewer sat down in a chair, put on glasses, and was literally placed on\nPandora, spaceships, and floating mountains. The viewer can feel so\nengulfed by the imagery, you feel like you can smell the leaves from\nthe trees. Avatar is utterly hypnotizing. James Horner's score is some\nof the best work done in his career. It offers a variable of\ndevastation that moves the viewer to near tears. It goes back to his\nwork on Titanic, where the musical instruments lifted the material\nimmensely. The entire sound team is also locked and loaded for Oscar\nrecognition as the feeling of animals, machines, and arrows buzzing by\nyour head leave you imprisoned in Cameron's exquisite film.

James Cameron has come back home ladies and gentlemen Cameron is back,\nbigger, badder, and mature in his crowning work of his career.\nTerminator 2: Judgment Day and Titanic do not even compare anymore.\nThis is the film that can blend the fans of those two films together\nand lock Cameron into your heart. He's a definite spoiler for a\ndirecting bid for the Academy Awards. You have admire the raw, natural\ntalent the man has. How could you ever conceive such an experience and\nput that much effort and work into it and have it pay off? The box\noffice success will surely keep him in the minds of voters for various\ncritics' awards. His screenplay, leaps and bounds better than 1997's\nBest Picture Winner, is primed, developed and ripe for the taking.\nThough, you do acquire the tacky and atypical dialogue you expect from\na science fiction director of this caliber, you can appreciate the\neffort and the honesty of it all. James Cameron is everything Michael\nBay wishes he was, to put it bluntly.

Avatar will bring also great actors putting their best foot forward\nsuch as Giovanni Ribisi, who is as underrated as they come. Michelle\nRodriguez who exudes sexy like any woman starring in a sci-fi epic.\nJoel Moore, showing his range outside of his comedic work in Dodgeball:\nAn Underdog Story. And the classy veteran actors, CCH Pounder and Wes\nStudi, who just simply don't work enough.

Avatar is one of the best films of the year. The most exciting,\nthrilling, and superb work you'll feast your eyes on in any theater\nthis century. Cinema, forever, will remember the benchmark that James\nCameron placed not only for himself, but for any man, daring to change\nthe game, the way Cameron did. Avatar is a movie experience to be\nremembered, and please experience in a movie theater first.

***½/****\n

How could anyone who's seen more than 4 movies in their life find this creative?

\n
\nAuthor:\nbenjamin-heckendorn from United States
\n20 December 2009
\n

*** This review may contain spoilers ***

\r\n\n
\n

\nLet me preface this by saying I am a huge James Cameron fan. I think\n"Terminator" is a masterpiece of sci-fi and the single best edited film\nof all time, "Aliens" is easily the best sequel not from the original\ndirector and I personally like it a lot more than the original. "Abyss"\ncreated an interesting world and was racked with tension, "Terminator\n2" is an action tour-de-force and has what I believe the best sound\nediting/design of all time, "True Lies" is goofy fun, more poignant\nthan most give it credit for with a boffo ending, and finally "Titanic"\nwas far better than anyone expected it to be and has one of the most\nbelievable romances in a movie.

So then there was the 12 year wait for "Avatar". No reviews, word of\nmouth, ticket price or natural disaster would keep me from the midnight\nIMAX 3D screening. I went with moderate expectations, which the film\nmatched exactly. While this kept me from being disappointed it wasn't a\ngood thing.

Movie fans were on edge because if "Avatar" flops, it'll mean the death\nof creativity in a remake-happy Hollywood. That's a valid concern but\nthe problem is nothing in "Avatar" is creative whatsoever. It is\nliterally "Dances with Wolves" in space, right down to having a "Wind\nin his Hair" character as a foil to the "new guy".

As the movie progressed it caused the worst of all bad reactions for me\nas a viewer - I started thinking about other things. Then I kept\nthinking "well the end battle is supposed to be awesome".

SPOILER WARNING! (if for some reason you can't guess how this movie\nends) End battle comes and the movie switches from being "Dances with\nWolves" to Disney's "Atlantis" (but that's unfair since both of those\nmovies contained far, FAR better musical scores) Gunship windows that\nwere impervious to arrows a few scenes ago (to show Na'vi desperation)\nsuddenly become brittle and break easily (to show Na'vi heroism). The\nbattle isn't nearly as long as you'd expect, and nowhere near as epic /\nengaging as the finale of Return of the King. Finally there's a\nRobo-suit vs Navi fight and it is admittedly pretty good. Unlike 95% of\ndirectors these days Cameron knows enough to pull the camera back and\nlet us SEE the fight. However it's an odd choice to spend millions on\n10 foot tall "creatures" and then rarely even have them in the same\nframe as humans.

And of course there's the obligatory scene where characters are dying\nin slow motion as Middle Eastern vocal music plays (see Gladiator or\nMunich) Wouldn't have been so bad if I hadn't said to my friend on the\nway there "How much you wanna bet there's a part where characters die\nin slow motion over Middle Eastern music?"

As with most movies these days "Avatar" is far too long, glossing over\nimportant details (why is Earth dying, what does Unobtainium do, etc)\nand instead spending time on flight scenes, mystic ceremonies, spirit\ntrees and nature bonding.

Ultimately I just did not care. Since the dawn of cinema Hollywood\nrecycles old ideas every few decades and slaps on a bigger budget / new\nstars / better effects so you won't notice. I can see re-doing "Ben\nHur" with color and sound, but why do we need "Dances with Wolves" with\n20 times the budget and blue Native Americans? No amount of impressive\nvisuals, robo-suits, gunship battles, perfect character CGI or Sigorney\nWeaver can change the fact that I've seen this movie 100 times before.\nI could even accept the weak story if there were any characters to give\na rat's ass about. This film is also dreadfully humorless, a stark\ncontrast to "Titanic" where humor was one of its many surprises.

Regarding 3D... I've seen many films in it now and honestly you don't\neven notice after the few minutes. It doesn't make the money any better\nor worse, just more expensive.

Citizen Kane, The Robe, Sound of Music, Jaws, Star Wars, Batman, Toy\nStory, The Matrix... these movies changed movies. We can say that\nlooking back now. "Avatar" saying it would ahead of time is pure\nhubris, "unsinkable Titanic"-like poppycock.

But if expensive motion-capture being used to cover up a weak, generic\nstory is the future of cinema then count me out, I'll stick with my\nPS3.\n

\n\n

The best blockbuster in years - the master is back!

\n
\nAuthor:\nftyl from www.clickonline.com
\n15 December 2009
\n\n
\n

\nIt has been 12 years since Cameron unleashed the phenomenon that was\nTitanic and real fans would have to look as far back as 1991's\nTerminator 2 for their last proper dose of his incredibly epic action\n(True Lies, while fun, really doesn't count). So the anticipation for\nAvatar has long since reached fever pitch and beyond.

Thankfully for the patient masses, Avatar has turned out to be the\nbiggest and best event movie of the year, perhaps the decade. The story\nis pure Cameron simplicity – a paraplegic ex-marine is given a chance\nto walk again through the use of a unique alien body, called an Avatar.\nIt is his job to gain the trust of the natives so that a greedy\ncorporation can steal the precious metal from their lush moon. Jake's\n(Sam Worthington) crippled main character is the perfect point of\ncontact for the audience – not only is he new to the visual delights of\nPandora but his disability means that every moment in his Avatar body\nis one of glorious freedom from the confinement of his chair. When the\nCorporations intentions become more sinister, Jake must choose between\nhis new found place with the natives and his own race and fight for\nwhat he believes in.

Avatar combines parts of Pocahontas and Braveheart with a liberal dose\nof Space Marines into an epic whole that takes nearly three full hours\nto unfold. We could criticise that length, the weak story and the hammy\ndialogue. We could attack its thinly-veiled ecological message or the\nfrankly bizarre spirituality in its second half but honestly nothing\ncan spoil the experience while you are enveloped in it. And a large\npart of that is down to the brilliant use of 3D – which is both subtle\nand incredibly effective. Til now, we have been making movies with 3D\nelements, Avatar is the first truly 3D film and might well prove to be\none of the most significant things to happen to blockbuster film-making\nsince Star Wars.

Cameron is also pushing the envelope with truly photo-real CG –\nsomething which has been promised for years but has finally been\ndelivered with Avatar. The interactions of the characters with the\nenvironment is incredible and the detail on the faces of the\nmotion-captured leads (Worthington and Star Trek's Zoe Saldana) bring\nthem to life. You will believe totally in their performances,\nrepresenting another quantum leap in tools which have rarely been used\nfor anything other than spectacle.

Make no mistake, Avatar is an important film from a technical\nstandpoint but it is also great entertainment. The world of Pandora is\na stunning spectacle from scene to scene and as Jake learns more about\nthe Na'vi the film approaches the kind of light hearted adventure story\nwhich has been absent from movie theatres for years. Then the final act\nexplodes into tragedy and desperate action, with the final half hour a\nblistering life or death struggle that has to be seen to be\ndisbelieved.

Over the coming days you will be hearing a lot about Avatar, and some\nof the critical reaction is bound to focus on its weaknesses in a bid\nto appear appropriately reserved and objective. But this is not a film\nto be dissected or examined, rather one to be experienced with a warm\ncrowd, a great sound system, in 3D as you bask in the knowledge that\nthe movie-making master is back!\n

Incredible scope and spectacle

\nA film with the scope of James Cameron's Avatar was always going to be\na risk both artistically and financially, especially in today's\neconomic climate. Whether it will pay off monetarily is a question only\ntime can answer, but this viewer can at the very least attest to it\nbeing an artistic triumph.

Avatar brings us as close as cinema ever has to actually visiting an\nalien world. The beautiful environs, the exotic creatures and\nincredibly lifelike natives of Pandora arrest the senses, visually,\naurally and emotionally. The world in Avatar is the true star of the\nshow. The amount of detail and work that has gone into bringing this\nnew world alive is seriously impressive, and it will be a while before\nwe see anything that overtakes it in scope and quality. WETA Workshop\nand ILM have truly outdone themselves.

Relative newcomer, Aussie Sam Worthington provides a solid human heart\namongst all the science-fiction/fantasy beauty and Zoe Saldana gives an\nimpressive performance as the 8 foot tall Na'vi, Neytiri. Even though\nthe characters they both play are blue, giant, catlike aliens, they\nmanaged to evoke a chemistry and likability that pierces through the\nspecial effects.

That's not to say that everything is perfect. The story is basic and\ndare I say, clichéd and predictable. We have seen it plenty of times in\nall forms of media. The bad guys are cartoonishly evil, and sadly paper\nthin. The love story, while charming, is also clichéd despite being\nbetween man and alien. But in the face of these shortcomings, Avatar is\na success because its storytelling lies in the brilliant visuals.

Avatar is a beautiful piece of film and a true event. It does exactly\nwhat cinema was always intended to - it takes us away from our problems\nand worries for a few hours and gives us memorable images which will\nundoubtedly and deservedly enter into the cultural lexicon to stay for\nthe foreseeable future.

9.5/10\n

Cameron Returns

\n

*** This review may contain spoilers ***

\nNobody directs like James Cameron. Ten years in the works, and every\nminute was worth it. It is the Star Wars of the 21st century. Though\nthe plot is derivative and bears some pretty close resemblance to\nDances with Wolves and a little bit of The Last Samurai, it does not\nruin a mind-blowing experience. Avatar has a great future ahead of it\nand, possibly another Oscar for the mighty James Cameron.

Jake Sully, is an ex-marine, bound to a wheel chair. He seeks to make a\nfresh start on the moon Pandora where his brother worked. The moon has\na military run mining colony. The business has brought humans into\ncontact with the local natives, tall blue dudes, called Na'vi. The\nNa'vi inhabit a part of the moon under which lies a large deposit of\nvaluable minerals, and the humans are trying to get them to relocate.\nColonel Quarich who runs the operation, ask Sully to go under cover as\na Na'vi, to learn the secrets, to give the humans an advantage. If\nsuccessful, Sully will get his legs back. The magic of future science\nand 3-D effects give Sully a handsome genetically engineered Avatar\nbody, and he embarks on his mission. Once on the inside however, his\nloyalties to the humans start to turn when his heart gets lost to the\nNa'vi princess Neytiri. Is he willing to fight on the blue side now?

The landscape of Pandora is the most creative and inventive of the\ndecade. There are creatures that you might find in your wildest dreams\nor your worst nightmares. There is flora and fauna that shine with the\nbioluminescence of a thousand deep sea critters, and also trees which\ndwarf the Empire State Building.

I guess the only noticeable problem with Avatar, is a lack of original\nstory. What you see on the screen will blow you away, but all the tough\ncritics, may come down a bit on the fact that despite being the latest\nin film technology, Cameron uses a very old archetypal formula, perhaps\njust a bit too schematic. There are a few badly scripted segments of\ndialogue, but nothing that makes you wanna blow raspberries at the\nscreen.

What is most important is the fact that Cameron's story gets the viewer\ninvolved and offers a handful of memorable and likable characters to\nroot for, and some to root against. This is the post- modern era, and\noriginality is rare, but if there is any technical progress to be made\nin the world of movies and entertainment, there is one name that dwarfs\nall JAMES CAMERON, and after ten years hiding from the Hollywood\nmainstream, he is back.\n
