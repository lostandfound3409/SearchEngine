Reading The Lord of the Rings Made Me Buy a Fitbit

\n\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\tPosted on March 19, 2015 by Rick MacDonnell @ Canon Fodder\t\t
\n\t\t\n\t\t\t\t

0

\n\t\t\t
\n\n\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t


\n 

\n

I\xe2\x80\x99ve always had a problem with remorseful smokers.

\n

Truthfully, I have an aversion to smokers in general (sorry, I find it disgusting), but it takes a particularly disturbing lack of willpower to acknowledge how harmful smoking is, hate yourself a little bit for being a smoker, and yet continue to be one.

\n

I try to keep in mind the habitual pull of addiction and how behaviors can be ingrained over time (especially ones established while young), but I just can’t come to terms with how an actual grown person\xe2\x80\x94who in all likelihood is at least moderately intelligent and self-possessed\xe2\x80\x94can have all the facts at their disposal and still put themselves, and the people around them, in harm’s way.

\n

Willpower is an incredibly valuable trait. Unconsciously, I use it as a measuring stick for how much I respect a person. Few things impress me more than someone who is able to ignore their biases, or the easy ways out, in order to make logical, more beneficial choices. Like avoiding delicious fatty foods in favor of healthier ones, choosing to skip a night out in order to finish something for work the next day, or deciding to give up smoking.

\n

Avoiding the easy way out is hard. Really hard, in some cases. Most of the time taking the easy way out is simply following our instincts, so to disregard that feeling in the name of doing what\xe2\x80\x99s right is impressive.

\n

Willpower affects how you treat people, whether you’re on time, how you perform at your job, whether you give in to obsessions, and a million other things. With these higher-level concerns, I think I\xe2\x80\x99ve done pretty well. I’ve never been a smoker. I don’t do drugs. I\xe2\x80\x99ve never fallen in with bad people or bad influences, gotten into destructive relationships, or committed any crimes. I treat people with kindness, sometimes even those who don\xe2\x80\x99t deserve it. My mother doesn\xe2\x80\x99t even have any stories of me \xe2\x80\x9cbeing bad\xe2\x80\x9d as a child.

\n

In the grand scheme of things, I\xe2\x80\x99m squeaky clean. Because I\xe2\x80\x99ve prided myself on being able to say no to things that aren\xe2\x80\x99t good for me in the long run. The end result is a life that’s pretty vanilla, but that\xe2\x80\x99s the cost of making good choices. Recently, though, I realized I’m not making nearly as many good choices as I thought.

\n

I’m in my 30s now. Despite the fact that I don\xe2\x80\x99t look it, I\xe2\x80\x99m woefully out of shape. I’ve talked about writing a novel for five years and I haven’t written a word. I eat like a college freshman. I’ve bailed on four different blogs in six years. I don\xe2\x80\x99t get into trouble because I don\xe2\x80\x99t really do anything. Essentially, I live like a sloth.

\n

So while I’m not addicted to drugs, punching people in the face, or doing dumb shit with even dumber people, I’m guilty of an embarrassing lack of willpower on a daily basis. In mundane matters, I’m completely ruled by my id. I mean, right after I write this I’m going to eat a pizza. There\xe2\x80\x99s a little person inside of me who\xe2\x80\x99s fat, sweaty, and rolls around on heely wheels because the sheer act of walking makes him wheezy.

\n

I hate it. My life isn\xe2\x80\x99t out of control by any means, but for someone who\xe2\x80\x99s taken so much pride in his own willpower, I\xe2\x80\x99m a total hypocrite. It\xe2\x80\x99s been a few months since I\xe2\x80\x99ve noticed it, and it\xe2\x80\x99s been a little hard to take, mostly because\xe2\x80\x94much like the smoker who can\xe2\x80\x99t quit\xe2\x80\x94I recognize my own destructive behaviors and I\xe2\x80\x99m still doing them. Every day.

\n

I thought I was better than this. If I ever had a problem, I always thought that I could get rid of it through sheer force of will. By myself. But then I realized that maybe I can\xe2\x80\x99t, and that made me sad.

\n

But then I started reading The Lord of the Rings, and things started to change.

The Beatles’ Lord of the Rings: the Best Movie Never Made?

\n\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\tPosted on March 2, 2015 by Rick MacDonnell @ Canon Fodder\t\t
\n\t\t\n\t\t\t\t

7

\n\t\t\t
\n\n\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t

“Some things, which should not have been forgotten, were lost.”

\n

\n

Even if you subscribe to the multiverse theory\xe2\x80\x94in which there are millions upon millions of universes existing simultaneously, each one\xc2\xa0different than the last\xe2\x80\x94it’s still\xc2\xa0almost impossible to imagine a reality wherein The Beatles starred in a live-action adaptation of\xc2\xa0The Lord of the Rings, directed by Stanley Kubrick. And yet, we were perilously, deliciously close to it being the one we’re in right now.

\n

It was 1967 and The Beatles were nearly as dominant at the box office as they were in record stores. The success of\xc2\xa0A Hard Day’s Night,\xc2\xa0Help!, and\xc2\xa0Yellow Submarine lent the band an unusually large leash (and budget) with their production company Apple Films, and they took advantage of it by pitching an adaptation of Tolkien’s\xc2\xa0international best-seller.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt was something John (Lennon) was driving,” said Peter Jackson, on a press tour for his\xc2\xa0original trilogy.

\n

Not surprisingly, this happened mere months before The Beatles dropped two of their most drug-induced albums,\xc2\xa0Sgt. Peppers’ Lonely Hearts Club Band and\xc2\xa0Magical Mystery Tour. Lennon was quoted that year as saying, “There are only about 100 people in the world who understand our music,” but only four understood why they should star in this movie: themselves.

\n

In Lennon’s pitch, Paul McCartney was assigned the role of Frodo (of course). Ringo would have taken on the role of Sam, and George Harrison would have donned Gandalf’s pointy hat. And Lennon? Unbelievably … and yet believably … he was set to play Gollum.
