Sterling, Guertin and band rocked Elton John songs

Chris Shields, clshields@stcloudtimes.com 10:24 a.m. CDT March 18, 2015

The artistry, the songwriting and the vocal and instrumental richness of Elton\'s early albums are often overlooked in favor of the greatest hits. But Sterling, Guertin and band brought gems to life.

Mick Sterling (left) and Peter Guertin brought their ELTONSONGS show to the Pioneer Place on Fifth on March 1. The Elton John tribute show touched on some hits but focused on deeper cuts.(Photo: Courtesy of Mick Sterling)

Sometimes all it takes is a really good concert to remind you how great an artist is.

My wife treated me to the March 1 show of "ELTONSONGS," an Elton John tribute concert by musicians Mick Sterling, Peter Guertin and a 10-piece band of top-notch musicians at St. Cloud\'s Pioneer Place on Fifth.

Tribute shows can be dicey affairs, especially when the artist being feted is one with such a voice, such a stage presence and so much variety of music. But Sterling, Guertin and the gang really did an amazing job with so many deep cuts. "Indian Sunset," "Skyline Pigeon," "The King Must Die" and "My Father\'s Gun" are just a handful of the many highlights (were there any lowlights at this show? I can\'t remember any!).

Sterling told the audience something along the lines of, "If we do nothing else today, I hope we encourage you to go out and buy \'Tumbleweed Connection\' and \'Madman Across the Water,\' " two of John\'s great early 1970s albums. Like Sterling, I hope many in the audience did just that. The artistry, the songwriting, and the vocal and instrumental richness of these albums so often get overlooked in favor of greatest-hits commercial songs.

At the intermission, my wife said how surprised she was about the depth and quality of Sir Elton\'s work. Sure, she knew about a lot of the big numbers. But "Indian Sunset" and those songs? Brand new experiences for her. "Did you know that song? Do you have that album?" Congrats folks, I think you\'ve made a new deep-cuts fan.

How did John and lyricist Bernie Taupin nail that kind of feeling down so well? Where did all that music come from? The very vibe, the soul of those songs comes from a place those youngsters couldn\'t have experienced yet. Still, the music was there. Those talented Brits. As Sterling remarked, sometimes it just isn\'t fair how talented some folks are. But hey, we benefit from their art, so I can\'t complain too much!

And as much as I\'m sure people want to hear songs like "Candle in the Wind," "Crocodile Rock" or even my personal favorite "Goodbye Yellow Brick Road," you can (and probably do) hear those songs everywhere. It was refreshing to hear mostly deeper cuts, especially from the earlier period. Album cuts aren\'t just filler, especially on the first several Elton John albums.

That\'s not to say some of the hits weren\'t performed. "Your Song," "Rocket Man (I Think It\'s Going to be a Long, Long Time)," "Philadelphia Freedom" and "Saturday Night\'s Alright for Fighting" all got airings (and were all done extremely well), but it was hearing songs like "Funeral For a Friend/Love Lies Bleeding" and "Have Mercy on the Criminal" that really blasted me into the stratosphere. Wow!

When Sterling started singing "Border Song," I was almost overwhelmed. This is one of my favorite songs from the "Elton John" record from 1970.

Sterling had just the right amount of smoke, soul and richness to his voice; he put across the power, the emotion of the song. Song? All of the songs. He took the lead on every track except "Madman Across the Water," which was handled by the fantastic Cate Fierro.

The love that the whole band had for this music was evident. The spot-on perfection of the drumming, the sublime bass work, the excellent vocals of the ladies on stage, brilliant acoustic and electric guitar playing, and exemplary keyboard performances (Guertin didn\'t use any sheet music, and he played the piano for every song performed) all added up to a dynamic, powerful and fulfilling show. The physical and emotional demands of some of this material put the performers\' stamina to the test, but again \xe2\x80\x94 the love of the material shone through. They never shied away from the big notes, the fast pieces or the long songs.

Simply put, this was one of the best tribute shows I\'ve ever seen. Heck, tribute shows? This was one of the best concerts I\'ve ever seen. Close your eyes, you\'d swear you were hearing Elton at the top of his game. Listen to the piano, feel the pulse of that bass down low, feel the snap of the drums and you\'re right there.

And I dare you not to sing along with the chorus of "Levon." I tried not to, but I couldn\'t help it. The performances catch you up in the music and lift you out of yourself. "He shall be Levon" indeed!

It was pure pleasure to attend this show, and it was a wonderful reminder of just how good those first several Elton John albums are. I greedily hope the band comes through on an ELTONSONGS2 show. Maybe they could bust out "Empty Garden (Hey Hey Johnny)" for me. Aw, heck, I\'d settle for any songs they want to put on stage. They\'ve got the talent, I trust them.

If you get a chance to catch Sterling, Guertin and crew doing an Elton John show, go. You will not be disappointed.

And I hope Pioneer Place on Fifth continues with the Sunday afternoon shows. This one sure hit the spot.

This is the opinion of music enthusiast and ELTONSONGS fan Chris Shields. Contact him at clshields@stcloudtimes.com and follow him on Twitter @clshields1980.
