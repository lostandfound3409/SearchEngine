Top 100 Children’s Novels #5: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe by C.S. Lewis

\n\t\t\n\t\t
June 21, 2012 By Elizabeth Bird
\t\t
\n\t\t\t

#5 The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe by C.S. Lewis (1950)
\n204 points

\n

The book that made me a reader, by a writer whose pure enthusiasm for life and story carries you on a lion’s back through the best of adventures. – Susan Van Metre

\n

I remember at my vast old age in 7th grade sadly concluding that I was too old for the Narnia books now. (I had already read them many times.) Then I took them up again in college and found new riches. I know I will never \xe2\x80\x9coutgrow\xe2\x80\x9d them again. No kid who reads this book will ever look at a closet door the same way again. – Sondra Eklund

\n

The first series I read to myself, starting halfway through when I switched from listening to my mom read them aloud, to sneaking them off to my room to read ahead. I was convinced that someday I would meet the Pevensies and tell them that I knew about Narnia, too. Sadly, Turkish Delight did not live up to my expectations. – Jessalynn Gale

\n

I still remember the day I finished this book, laying on my parent\xe2\x80\x99s family room couch on a bright, sunny summer day. I would have been playing outside in the sprinkler had I been able to put it down. Instead I was SOBBING on the couch as Aslan died. I finished it and read it again. And again. I don\xe2\x80\x99t always think the oldest, most classic version of a tale is the one that kids should keep rending. If someone else comes along and does the tale better, by all means, let\xe2\x80\x99s read that one\xe2\x80\xa6 but has anyone done this better? – Nicole Johnston Wroblewski

\n

I remember a sense of magic while reading the Chronicles of Narnia as a child. And I\xe2\x80\x99m not referring to the magic contained in the storylines. But rather the giddy awe of falling into the story. It was thrilling. It’s a very specific emotion, one I don\xe2\x80\x99t think we have a word for, one I don’t think I’ve ever felt as an adult — but it’s an emotion that I remember perfectly. The characters and worlds seemed so alive. I think it\xe2\x80\x99s one of the few times I really felt transported to another place through the pages of a book. And being the Chronicles of Narnia, that\xe2\x80\x99s rather fitting. – Aaron Zenz

\n

The synopsis from the publisher reads, “When Peter, Susan, Edmund and Lucy are sent to stay with a kind professor who lives in the country, they can hardly imagine the extraordinary adventure that awaits them. It all begins one rainy summer day when the children explore the Professor\xe2\x80\x99s rambling old house. When they come across a room with an old wardrobe in the corner, Lucy immediately opens the door and gets inside. To her amazement, she suddenly finds herself standing in the clearing of a wood on a winter afternoon, with snowflakes falling through the air. Lucy has found Narnia, a magical land of Fauns and Centaurs, Nymphs and Talking Animals \xe2\x80\x94 and the beautiful but evil White Witch, who has held the country in eternal winter for a hundred years.”

\n

According to 100 Best Books for Children by Anita Silvey (do you own your copy yet?) when Lewis was sixteen he envisioned a faun carrying an umbrella in a wood full of snow.\xc2\xa0 “Then nine years later, a lion leapt into a story, and Lewis began working on a book entitled \xe2\x80\x98The Lion\xe2\x80\x99.”\xc2\xa0 I was unaware that he was only twenty-five when he began the tale.\xc2\xa0 He\xe2\x80\x99d be fifty-two by the time it published, though.\xc2\xa0 That\xe2\x80\x99s what we call in the business a gestation period.\xc2\xa0 He did show an early manuscript to one Roger Lancelyn Green, though, and Green helped him get his manuscript up to snuff.\xc2\xa0 The book was originally meant to stand alone, which is part of the reason it bugs me when publishers release the books in the order of what happens in the series rather than the order of when the books were written.

\n

Of course, he was buds with J.R.R. Tolkien (though perhaps “buds” is not the term they might choose to describe their friendship).\xc2\xa0 Tolkien wasn\xe2\x80\x99t a fan of the series though.\xc2\xa0 Considering he was a fellow who spent ages constructing a history and a bloody language for his fantastical world, he found the whole Narnia thing a bit slapdash.

\n

Now if you walk into the book as a kid and aren\xe2\x80\x99t aware that you\xe2\x80\x99re facing a great big gigantic Christian allegory, you probably won\xe2\x80\x99t notice it anyway.\xc2\xa0 For adults, it\xe2\x80\x99s incredibly obvious.\xc2\xa0 Still, as Anita Silvey says, “The books have endured not because of their philosophy, but because they bring to life a magical world that readers want to enter again and again.”

\n

Philip Pullman?\xc2\xa0 Not a fan.\xc2\xa0 In an interview with surefish.com, for example, he says, “Narnia has always seemed to me to be marked by a hatred of the physical world. When I bring this up, people say, oh no, what nonsense! He loved his beer, loved laughter and smoking a pipe, and the companionship of his friends and so on. And so he might have done. But that didn\xe2\x80\x99t prevent perhaps his unconscious mind from saying something quite different in the form of a story.”

\n

I can\xe2\x80\x99t even wade through the thousands of scholarly articles on this book, or even the BOOKS based on it.\xc2\xa0 I will highlight one, though.\xc2\xa0 In 2008 Little, Brown published Salon co-founder Laura Miller\xe2\x80\x99s title The Magician\xe2\x80\x99s Book: A Skeptic\xe2\x80\x99s Adventures in Narnia.\xc2\xa0 In it she records how, “My relationship to Narnia would turn out to be as rocky as any love affair, a story of enchantment, betrayal, estrangement, and reunion.”\xc2\xa0 In fact Miller allowed a section from the Introduction of the book to be included in Anita Silvey\xe2\x80\x99s Everything I Need to Know I Learned from a Children\xe2\x80\x99s Book.\xc2\xa0 She writes, “In one of the most vivid memories from my childhood, nothing happens.\xc2\xa0 On a clear, sunny day, I\xe2\x80\x99m standing near a curb in the quiet suburban California neighborhood where my family lives, and I\xe2\x80\x99m wishing, with every bit of myself, for two things.\xc2\xa0 First, I want a place I\xe2\x80\x99ve read about in a book to really exist, and, second, I want to be able to go there.\xc2\xa0 I want this so badly I\xe2\x80\x99m pretty sure the misery of not getting it will kill me.\xc2\xa0 For the rest of my life, I will never want anything quite so badly again.”

\n

I\xe2\x80\x99ve yet to find any statues of the characters from the book, but there is a statue of the wardrobe out there.\xc2\xa0 In East Belfast, Northern Ireland you can see this figure of what some folks are calling Digory Kirke a.k.a. The Professor moving into the wardrobe.

\n

\n

Whether Digory was Lewis\xe2\x80\x99s alter ego is up for contention.

\n

Big names have a tendency to illustrate these covers.\xc2\xa0 Chris Van Allsburg.\xc2\xa0 David Wiesner.\xc2\xa0 Folks you wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t necessarily associate with jackets as a job.\xc2\xa0 And in a surprising move the publisher released the book recently in its original jacket.\xc2\xa0 This is a trend I approve of, though it only works for the true classics.\xc2\xa0 Which is to say, most of the books on this list.\xc2\xa0 Here are some covers:

In terms of film, as crummy as this looks I was rather fond of this old Wonderworks version of the story back in the day.\xc2\xa0 I think I blocked out the poorly animated portions, though.\xc2\xa0 Guh.

\n

\n

The most recent (and best) version.\xc2\xa0 Some folks complained at the time that the movie was trying to be the next Lord of the Rings.\xc2\xa0 I figure the only reason they even made it was because Lord of the Rings had proved you could make money off such a film.

\n

Before the first Narnia movie produced by Walden Media came out they had a special screening for some of the kids at the Jefferson Market Branch of NYPL.\xc2\xa0 They showed a long trailer for the kids that is now, I see, on YouTube.\xc2\xa0 I present it to you now.

About A Fuse #8 Production
